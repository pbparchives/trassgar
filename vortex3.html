Breaking camp at mid-morning, the halfling and she-elf loaded up their mounts in thoughtful silence.

After a hasty meal of burnt rabbit the night previous, they had watched with respect as their wounded dwarven comrade disappeared into the dusty forest darkness on his pony. Gnash had insisted that Amber and Tilder continue the trek to the Highlands without him; in a week he would catch up with them using his boots of speed. Amber had no doubt the resourceful dwarf made it back before midnight to Forest's Edge for some needed recuperation. "And brew!" he had been quick to add with gusto. Oh, the songs that must have been sung at Klive's tavern last night! When she next saw the innkeeper, she'd have to compare stories to see how much Gnash had embellished. She already missed the good-natured dwarf.

Alas, personal feelings had to be subordinate to the quest; loyalty clashed with destiny. She could handle traveling without Gnash for the time being, but she would also have to forego investigating Tilder's wild claim. How long could she wait? After monumental search efforts undertaken by several houses for over a year, the Rangers of the Seventh had been given up for dead; the possibility (however faint after two years) that they still lived might renew lagging spirits in the aligned houses, bring them out of self-imposed isolation and perhaps unite them all against Klaxton Kurl. But so would recovering the St'eelcoil, in theory.

Amber's mare whinnied impatiently. The she-elf peered over at Tilder, already in the saddle of his pony. His gaze was fixed on Gnash's path. The thief's dexterous fingers tied random and complex knots in his pony's mane. This halfling defies explanation, she thought. How could a common thief from such a simple race come across such sought-after knowledge? And then there was that eerie moment two nights ago when he had immediately recognized her as royalty and had suggested that she might know him---that she might have reason to reprimand him.

These facts could be explained, as she was beginning to suspect, by a connection with THoR, but how probable was that? The elves of Trassgar don't generally associate with halflings, much less recruit them into their ranks. "History just has too much momentum," she remembered Scurge saying. She laughed inwardly at the irony of the quest parameters as researched by Scrub Shack. Perhaps elven schisms might not be the only ones to heal when the Vortex curse is lifted. Ever since the incident with M'ylin, elves and halflings had not had much good to do with each other. If Tilder had been granted access to privileged information through THoR, he must indeed be a singular halfling, she surmised. Or a treacherous thief.

"We'd best get a move on, Tilder. I want to make it to Two Rivers' Forge a few days before Gnash arrives. We'll need time to obtain fresh mounts and rest up before we three tackle the road to Aquus Keep." And perhaps along the way, you might feel inclined to talk, eh, halfling? Amber did not emit her last thought as she climbed up into the saddle. For now at least, she would respect Tilder's silence.

Tilder straightened in his saddle and gestured politely for Amber to lead the way. He'd been trying to hide the fact that he felt uncomfortable around Amber, but he suspected she knew. It was a feeling he could not quite justify. Soon after they had passed the trio of fresh graves, he ruled out the possibility that it might be due to her forwardness and insistence on picking his brain. No, his unease was rooted deeper than that.

The thief was thankful that Amber was content to lead a few pony-lengths ahead. It gave him opportunity to ponder his anxiety. As the forested glen broadened into a flat featureless semi-arid plain, Tilder cradled his emotion and noted its simultaneous novelty and familiarity. Yet he dared not examine it too closely. He didn't really need to ask why, did he? Just pass it off to on-the-quest-jitters, that's right, you dumb ol' thief. Don't mean a thing....

But the steady gait of his pony and the monotony of the scene eventually took their toll on his mental restraint. Trassgar's twin suns, locked in their lofty barycentric embrace, shined down relentlessly on the questers. Way down there on the path to Two Rivers' in the middle of the vast plain, the semi-hypnotic halfling on the scruffy pony got his answer: dreaded parallels between past and present manifested themselves again.

Jop resumed in his normal booming voice, "Yeah, so you got that, dolt? Take this report to the big K. And don't get lost this time, cheese-head!" Jop shoved his first officer away with a neat flick of his meaty hand. On any other night, Tilder would have noted carefully that Jop's swipe would have knocked a Hairfoot like himself senseless. But not this night. Hidden in the shadows cast by the thick mul-pine, Tilder squinted in deep concentration. His mind raced to ingrain the vital directions he'd just obtained from a successful reading of Jop's lips.

Tarc picked himself up and straightened his leather armor. He cracked his orcish knuckles, slid the rolled parchment into his satchel, and headed uphill. The wrong way to Klaxton Kurl, Tilder noted with a smirk. Then it dawned upon him: Tarc's heading towards Rowend! But his view of the departed black sasher was now eclipsed by Bork, who was sauntering back to his seat with a leaky mug of mead. Without a smidgen of finesse, the drunken orc flopped down heavily against the trunk of Tilder's mul-pine and braved the ensuing assault of liberated pinecones.

Tilder was still biting his nails and straining his eyes uphill waiting for the inevitable confrontation, when into the firelight Tarc reappeared, his face a blanched green. The thief's heart jolted with anticipation. Finty! He's seen something!

"What now, Tuskless?" taunted Jop, exasperated. The other sashers looked in Tarc's direction, sensing a good laugh, but the situation turned serious as Jop rose to his feet, unsheathed his scimitar and walked slowly toward his shuddering first officer. Tarc started babbling in fear and pointing uphill, but Jop silenced him with a quick sweep of his blade across the officer's face. The sasher campsite was deathly still.

"Downhill, fool." With a guttural growl, Jop wiped his weapon on Tarc's leather shoulder armor and walked back to his seat.

He wondered what Ellendale had shown the poor orc. A shadow dragon, perhaps? He nearly giggled with relief.

Amber studied the halfling closely. Hmm, none of the usual signs. He's a good liar. But could this mean he's been telling the truth all along? She prodded no further. Evidently, his demons were not quite finished yet. She hoped whatever was left of him would be salvageable. The quest required it.

"Oh, I don't bite unless provoked. But let's continue to move. There's a spring not too far from here, and our four-footed friends need a drink. I'll lead."

Elf, halfling, and horses, having been refreshed from a visit to the trickle of water Amber had expertly found using her well-honed elven and ranger skills, the troupe continued in a northerly direction toward Two Rivers' Forge. The town ahead sat surrounding the split of the Lanagg river into its northern and southestern components, pinpointed by the juncture it formed with the Furter River. The two rushing currents wound south from their disparate sources, and met at the fertile valley upon which Amber and Tilder's horses now trod. Two Rivers' Forge, originally settled by a collective of humans who farmed the irrigated valley, became a hotbed of activity when a disgruntled dwarven family migrated from the furnaces of Krom, and taught the humans how to shape the metals of the land.

Now, Two Rivers' Forge boasted the hottest furnaces and finest craftsmen in northern Trassgar. At least the inhabitants were quick to make those claims, Amber related to Tilder. Each knew of the town and had traveled to it on occasion, but Amber took this opportunity to share the "hidden history" of the town--knowledge imparted to her by none other than the displaced dwarf, Gnashuphagnewtogius Gnarly.

"In fact," Amber joked, "Gnash claims to have been part of that disgruntled family of dwarves that got this place on the map." She grinned, and noticed that her companion smiled, too.

"I have decided that there's more to Gnash and his adventures than anyone knows," Amber finished.

"We all have our own mysterious pasts," Tilder said, barely aloud. Amber nodded slowly, and decided not to press Tilder for the history that seemed to haunt him.

"Yes," came Amber's only audible reply.

The two sat on their horses, on a small rise in the terrain, which gave them a good view of the town that spread out before them. The path they had chosen to follow was well traveled by traders and other folk making their ways north or south through northern Trassgar. One such traveler heading south was a human rider on the back of a large horse. He carried a wooden sign in his gloved hands, and had a white cloth tied around his head which covered his nose and mouth.

The rider slowed on his approach, and dismounted abruptly. He addressed the pair in a bold accent typical of the northern human settlements: "I see I've come just in time to warn the two of you." An onlooker to this gathering might have found it odd not only that an elf and a halfling traveled together in peace, but that the human newcomer didn't seem to pay much attention to the unlikely pair. The human's horse grunted and snorted at having been led so close to a foreign mare and pony. Amber and Tilder's mounts responded in kind, kicking the dust at their feet.

"Whoah there, buddy!" Tilder half-whispered, patting his pony's neck. He squinted to read the print on the sign as the man carried the two pieces of wooden boards to a distance marking that was chiseled into a roundly shaped rock at the edge of the path. Retrieving a mallet from a leather loop in his pants, the partially masked man gave the sign post two sturdy knocks which fixed the sign firmly in the dirt. His horse waited patiently.

Amber was content to let the man finish his task before inquiring. She, too, read the sign, and as the man stepped back to view his effort, he noticed that the writing on the sign had captivated the attention of the two would-be visitors to his town.

"You read the common tongue? Good." The man walked back to his horse, and tugged on the stirrups of the saddle. "Good, as I won't have to read it for you. I'd take it seriously, folks. Two Rivers' Forge isn't for tourists. It won't be for a few days." He stared at the elf, then the halfling, as they finished reading the sign and then looked at each other.

Amber's expression had turned to pity. Tilder's brow had wrinkled and his lip was quivering. Visibly shaken, Tilder broke the silence. "How. . . how many?"

"We're still counting. Twenty, so far, come down from the Furter. We're taking them out of the current, obviously, hence the notice." The man shook his head. "I got. . . I havta get back. Understand, normally, I'd say, 'Welcome to the Forge!' Not today." He all but pointed a finger at the two travelers. "You folks mind me, now." With that said, he climbed back onto his horse, a brown stallion, and wheeled him around back to the town.

He took a slow pace; Amber understood why.

The sign was hastily constructed, with letters etched into the wood and filled with a black ink, some of which had bled over the cut edges of the letters:
WARNING: TWO RIVERS' FORGE TO BE
AVOIDED! POTENTIAL PLAGUE VICTIMS
ARRIVE FROM UP RIVER. ENTER AT OWN RISK!
     
     
     
     
     

Tilder and Amber let their horses rest a minute, then looked at each other to decide what they were going to do.

Amber had that look on her face. What had Amalthea called it? Clamming. The unflappable elven ranger would roll her eyes skyward and jokingly chide the heavens whenever Rowend clammed---this usually meant THoR was in for it, courtesy of another of his trademark gut instincts. His last one was right on the electrum: they had discovered a band of marauding, monk-killing black-sashers. Oddly enough, it was not Rowend, but rather Amalthea herself, whose dare had led to the catastrophe that ended THoR's mission two years ago. Tilder shivered under Amber's blank stare. "Elves!" came the silent oath. "The trouble I get into because of elves!" But he knew they were only part of a larger equation dominated by his own actions... and Finty's cursed dice.

Tilder's attention shifted to the plague warning and more gloomy thoughts. The black ink that filled the bottommost letters of the sign was still semi-viscous, and some of it had dripped onto the distance marker below, marring the smooth stone with sickly splotches. "Those poor people." He suddenly felt itchy. "So much for meeting Gnash at Two Rivers', eh?" The air swallowed his words on their way to Amber.

Indeed, Amber had not registered the halfling's timid banter. Her thoughts were elsewhere, deeply sundered with indecision. Given such a chance on any other mission, she might have backtracked to the Black Forest to investigate Tilder's wild claims. The legendary Rangers of the Seventh! What a difference they could make in the land's struggle for freedom from Kurl's sashers! But another legend promised to wash away Kurl's scourge when the St'eelcoil was returned to the Vortex, or so Shack's theory went. Amber had always been the practical sort, trusting honed reflexes and tempered steel over arcane magic and superstition, but nevertheless, bolstered by a close fit to the legend parameters described by Shack, she could feel the urgent tug of destiny. "Alas, if the Rangers have lasted this long, they can last a bit longer. Hang on, guys."

Tilder's incessant fidgeting finally forced her return to the present. "Surely you...you are not thinking of going down there?" he stammered.

Amber broke trance and dismounted. In the same fluid movement, she unsheathed the St'eelcoil from her mare's saddle and swung it swiftly around to point gem first at Tilder's bulbous nose. Tilder gasped with fear and nearly jumped off his pony, but he quickly managed his astonishment and balance. The scepter's rough-hewn gem, encircled by a metallic trefoil knot, captured the light of the twin suns and glinted painfully. Amber only made it worse by rotating the scepter in his face. The thief squinted, oblivious to the random spears of refracted sunlight dancing across the contours of his visage.

"Uh, we could leave a note for Gnash and meet him upstream at Aquus Keep," Tilder offered, spouting the first alternative that sprang to mind. Anything to break the tense moment, and Amber's eccentric behavior. He nervously scratched the hair on the back of his left hand. "Or we could double ba---"

"Tell me something, halfling. What do you do with a plague victim?" Amber's question came without warning.

"I... I don't understand, your maj---er, uh, Amber, yeah."

Still twirling the St'eelcoil in the thief's face, Amber briefly smiled at the insistence of Tilder's manners. "The bodies, what do you do with..." Amber stopped mid-sentence as soon as Tilder comprehended.

"You burn them."

"Precisely. Why would these victims have appeared so?" Amber paused. "See if this makes sense, good thief. Plague doesn't happen anywhere: it occurs where there is habitation. Furthermore, it takes time for plague to become epidemic: there is plenty of time to impose containment measures. Such quarantines are instated to prevent the spread to other settlements, and less ostensibly, to minimize the risk of retaliation by neighboring towns for lack of quarantine. Now the town below is a superbly armed community. How could a town upstream not burn its plague victims or fail to keep its walking dead within its own walls and not expect exacting retribution by the weaponsmiths downstream?"

"Maybe they wanted to burn the bodies on the other side of the river and the transport barge sank?" Tilder interjected as he satisfied an ankle itch against a stirrup strap. His pony shook its mane.

"Hmm. Possibly, but why risk breaking quarantine by crossing the river, when you could have just burned the bodies in town? It just doesn't appear likely. The implied scenario: 'a town helplessly quarantined because of a blunder upstream' just doesn't add up. I smell a conveniently crafted coverup. Two Rivers' Forge holds a secret, and they want to keep it that way."

Oh no. Tilder knew what was coming. Elves could smell trouble before it happened, and like a moth to the flame, they were irresistably drawn to it---at least the elves he'd known. He twisted around, unbuckled his pack and fished around in its contents as Amber continued.

"Taking another route, as you suggest, would lessen the chance of Gnash catching up with us. We've got to stick to our original plan. The fate of this land depends on upon us, wouldn't you ag---"

At this, Amber couldn't help but burst out in uncontrollable laughter. Tilder had swaddled himself up to the eyeballs in a faded indigo blanket, evidently in anticipation of entering plague country. The blanket looked like it belonged to Olen's bed back at Highsafe. She briefly wondered how her friends were doing. But Tilder's struggle to cover every mote of his skin with the generous blanket of dubious origin realigned her thoughts. She recovered briefly. "I'll take that as a 'yes'... but---ha!---you look like---a dried gooseberry!"

"Mmmf," replied the dried gooseberry.

"Ah, good point. There's no use letting on that we've called their bluff. Just make sure you can breathe, Tilder!" Amber donned a silk scarf over her mouth and nose, and then the pair raced to catch the sign poster before he entered the gates of the quarantined city.
